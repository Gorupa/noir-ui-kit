Abstract

This article examines the lived realities and overlapping marginalities of local women in and around Udaipur — focusing on the widow of an ex‑army officer, members of the transgender community, tribal women, and women entrepreneurs — to identify processes of social erasure and the forms of radical service, resistance, and care that sustain these women and their communities. The analysis draws on secondary literature, policy documents, and anonymized participant vignettes to situate localized stories within broader structural and policy frameworks. The study highlights pathways for more inclusive local governance, reparative welfare, and culturally responsive programs that centre dignity and agency.

Keywords: gender, transgender, ex‑service families, tribal women, Udaipur, social inclusion, grassroots leadership

Introduction

Local Lives, National Questions

This article centers women who are frequently omitted from mainstream histories and policy narratives: the widow of an ex‑service officer living in peri‑urban Udaipur, members of the local transgender community, tribal women from nearby panchayats, and women entrepreneurs who sustain household economies. By analyzing the intersections of gender, caste, service‑family status, and gender identity in one locality, the paper argues for integrated, culturally attuned interventions that respond to lived vulnerability and local forms of leadership.

The approach foregrounds stories as evidence: anonymized vignettes derived from local accounts are used to illustrate systemic gaps and the everyday innovations women use to claim dignity. The aim is policy-relevant: presentable to international journals while remaining rooted in local specificity.

Literature Review

Framing social erasure and grassroots service

Contextual review

Concepts and evidence

Scholarship on social erasure highlights how institutional practices and cultural narratives render certain groups invisible, limiting access to rights and recognition. Studies on ex‑service families emphasize financial precarity, bureaucratic barriers to benefits, and mental health impacts that disproportionately affect dependents, especially women left to manage households after a servicemember’s discharge or death.

Research on transgender communities in India documents legal progress alongside persistent stigma and exclusion from social protection and labour markets, with strong regional variation in lived outcomes. Indigenous and tribal women face layered marginalities due to land dispossession, limited representation in governance forums, and constrained access to credit and markets. Meanwhile, scholarship on women entrepreneurs in small towns highlights resilience strategies: collective savings groups, local market networks, and informal mentoring that allow businesses to survive despite scarce formal support.

Methodology

Design and sources

Secondary research & vignettes

Sources and ethical framing

This study relies primarily on secondary sources (policy reports, peer‑reviewed literature, government schemes) and anonymized participant vignettes. Vignettes are constructed to reflect recurring patterns observed in local reporting and community accounts; they are not verbatim transcripts and are labelled as pseudonyms to preserve confidentiality.

Ethical considerations included anonymization, sensitivity to trauma in accounts involving bereavement, and careful framing to avoid sensationalising the lives of marginalized people. The analysis interprets local examples in light of national policy frameworks to generate actionable recommendations.

Case Studies

Three local stories

Udaipur & surrounding panchayats
Portraits anonymized for safety

1. The ex‑service officer’s wife (Pseudonym: Meera)

Meera (pseudonym) is the widow of a retired army havildar who returned home with chronic health issues and limited pension documentation, and who later died after protracted illness. Left as the primary caregiver and breadwinner in a small peri‑urban settlement near Udaipur, Meera navigated complex bureaucratic processes to access widow’s pension and other entitlements. Her experience illustrates common barriers: unclear documentation requirements, delays in benefit disbursal, and limited local legal support for claims management.

Despite these hurdles, Meera organised a small ration‑club with neighbours to pool purchasing power, joined a self‑help group (SHG) to access microcredit, and started a home‑based tailoring unit that supplies local markets. These grassroots adaptations showcase how ex‑service family members transform precarity into collective forms of security, yet remain under‑recognized in local welfare planning.

2. Transgender community members (Pseudonyms: Rani and Aman)

Rani and Aman (pseudonyms) are two people from the local transgender community who made divergent livelihood choices: Rani works intermittently in local hospitality and as a ritual performer at private ceremonies; Aman engages in small‑scale trading and peer support work with an NGO that assists transgender youth. Both face stigma that limits formal employment opportunities and access to health services, despite recent legal recognition and some state-level schemes aimed at transgender welfare.

The community has formed informal mutual‑aid networks to cover healthcare costs and subsidize legal name‑change procedures; however, these networks are fragile and depend on episodic donor funding. The accounts show how legal recognition is necessary but insufficient without active local implementation and targeted livelihood support.

3. Tribal women and local entrepreneurs (Pseudonyms: Laxmi and Savita)

Laxmi (pseudonym) is a tribal woman from a nearby gram panchayat who cultivates millets on marginal land and participates in a cooperative that markets value‑added products. Her cooperative access remains constrained by limited cold‑storage, seasonal market access, and intermittent state procurement programmes. Laxmi’s cooperative involvement, however, has increased her bargaining power in local supply chains and enabled community investments in sanitation and child education.

Savita (pseudonym) runs a small grocery and tea stall in Udaipur’s suburban market, employing two women from her neighbourhood. She sources stock through informal wholesalers and has recently begun digital bookkeeping after assistance from an NGO training program. Savita’s entrepreneurship shows that micro‑enterprises can be engines of local employment if they receive better access to finance, market information, and digital literacy support.

Discussion

Findings and thematic synthesis

Patterns across cases

Common barriers and local innovations

Across cases, three cross-cutting themes emerge: administrative invisibility, constrained livelihood pathways, and resilient collective practices. Administrative invisibility appears where documentation, caste or gendered norms, and physical distance from welfare offices create de facto exclusion. Constrained livelihoods reflect limited formal employment opportunities, discriminatory hiring, and inadequate social protection. Resilient collective practices include SHGs, cooperatives, and informal mutual aid that both mitigate risks and create local leadership spaces for women.

Implications for recognition and policy

These findings suggest that legal recognition (e.g., transgender rights) or nominal entitlements (e.g., ex‑service pensions) must be paired with accessible local implementation, outreach for documentation support, and flexible financing for micro‑enterprises. Interventions that fail to address local administrative bottlenecks and community capacities will underperform.

Policy Implications

Recommendations for local and national actors

Actionable steps

1. Strengthen documentation and outreach

Local governments should establish mobile documentation clinics for ex‑service families, transgender persons seeking ID updates, and tribal communities needing land or beneficiary records. These clinics can reduce the transactional costs of accessing entitlements and improve timely benefit delivery.

2. Invest in intermediary institutions

Support to SHGs and cooperatives should include capacity building in digital bookkeeping, market linkages, and quality standards for value‑added goods. Small, targeted grants for storage and transport can materially increase incomes for tribal producers and women micro‑entrepreneurs.

3. Design culturally competent welfare pathways

Policy design must respect cultural practices while preventing exclusion: for instance, cash transfers for transgender individuals should be disbursed through trusted local organizations that can mediate stigma and provide follow‑up support for health and livelihoods.

Conclusion

From local stories to scalable approaches

Closing reflections

Concluding remarks

The local stories from Udaipur reveal durable forms of care and leadership among women who must navigate intersecting marginalities. Policy that centres administrative accessibility, intermediary institution strengthening, and culturally competent delivery can both recognise and amplify these grassroots practices.

For international audiences, the case of Udaipur illustrates how global concerns — gender inclusion, rights recognition, and inclusive livelihoods — are instantiated locally, and how local experiments in mutual aid and cooperative enterprise may offer transferable lessons.
